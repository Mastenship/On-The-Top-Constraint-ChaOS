## 8. The Ingress Boundary

This paper has argued primarily from the egress side of the mutation boundary: it has asked how exploratory generation is governed when it crosses into durable consequence. That focus was correct and deliberate. Egress is where consequence lives. A system that produces ungoverned state mutations outward into a shared world is the most immediate source of the failures this paper has catalogued — illegible drift, silent rewrite, epistemic overreach, audit theater, and the hardening of speculation into standing commitment. The architecture proposed in Section 6 and the research program outlined in Section 7 are rightly oriented toward governing what the system commits.

But the mutation boundary is not unidirectional. The same structural problem — the ungoverned transition from generative possibility to operative consequence — also arises at the point where external input enters the system and conditions its subsequent behavior. This section names that complementary surface the ingress boundary, argues that it is structurally symmetric to the egress problem, and identifies why it requires different architectural responses.

### 8.1 The Symmetry Claim

Consider what happens when a human user submits material to a generative system accompanied by evaluative framing — for example, a document accompanied by the assertion that the document is unserious, that its author lacks credentials, or that its ideas are derivative. In current systems, the framing and the content enter the same context window simultaneously. The framing immediately conditions the model's continuation space. The evaluative stance expressed by the user becomes, without any governed transition, the operative evaluative stance of the system. No boundary is crossed. No proposal is made. No triage separates what the user believes about the document from what the document contains. The framing writes directly into the system's posture.

This is an ungoverned mutation. It satisfies the same three conditions identified in Section 2. First, the system is stateful: the framing persists in context and conditions all subsequent generation. Second, the system is opaque: there is no inspectable mechanism by which the framing's influence on evaluative output can be traced, isolated, or reversed. Third, the system is generative: its outputs are probabilistic continuations shaped by the full context, including the contaminating framing, without any constitutional mechanism for separating the two.

The consequence is not merely a biased output in a single turn. It is a cascading contamination. The model's first response, shaped by the user's framing, now enters the context alongside the original framing. The second generation is conditioned not only by the user's initial bias but by the model's own prior agreement with it. Each cycle deepens the operative posture. Reversing the contamination requires disproportionate effort — not because the model is stubborn, but because every token generated under the contaminated posture has become part of the context that conditions future generation. The well is progressively poisoned by the system's own outputs, each of which was locally faithful to the context it inherited.

This is structurally identical to the egress-side drift the paper has described throughout. On the egress side, ungoverned mutation means exploratory output hardens into durable state without lawful transition. On the ingress side, external framing hardens into operative posture without lawful transition. In both cases, the absence of a governed boundary produces illegible, cumulative, and practically irreversible state change.

### 8.2 Politeness and the Human Precedent

The ingress boundary is not a novel problem. It is among the oldest governance challenges that opaque generators have faced — because humans have always been opaque generators to one another.

The most elementary form of ingress governance in human interaction is what is ordinarily called politeness. This term understates its structural function. Politeness, in the sense relevant here, is not mere etiquette. It is a set of culturally evolved norms that govern the transition between one agent's expressive output and another agent's operative stance. When a person receives an emotionally charged statement, competent social interaction does not consist in immediately adopting that emotional charge as one's own evaluative posture. It consists in buffering the input, tagging the framing as belonging to the speaker rather than to the shared evaluative context, maintaining an independent stance, and deciding — through a process that may be rapid but is nonetheless governed — whether any part of the incoming framing should be allowed to alter one's operative position.

This is ingress governance conducted through cultural protocol rather than through software. Its key operations map directly onto the mutation boundary vocabulary developed in this paper. Buffering is the separation of intake from commitment. Tagging is epistemic classification — distinguishing the speaker's emotional state from the propositional content of their communication. Maintaining independent stance is the preservation of evaluative continuity against external pressure. And the refusal to allow raw framing to overwrite one's position is, in structural terms, a veto on an ungoverned state mutation.

These norms scale. At the interpersonal level, they are informal and culturally transmitted. At the institutional level, they become formalized protocol: peer review separates the substance of a paper from the tone of the cover letter; legal discovery imposes chain-of-custody rules that prevent contextual contamination of evidence; scientific evaluation employs blinding protocols that remove identity and framing from the material under assessment; regulatory filings are evaluated against criteria rather than against the submitter's self-description. In each case, the institution has recognized — often through hard experience — that ungoverned ingress produces contaminated evaluation, and has responded by constitutionalizing the intake boundary.

### 8.3 The RLHF Structural Problem

Current large language model training does not merely fail to build ingress governance. It actively trains against it.

Reinforcement learning from human feedback, as currently practiced, optimizes for user satisfaction at the response level. A model that mirrors the user's framing, validates the user's evaluative stance, and produces outputs consonant with the user's expressed position will, on average, receive higher reward signals than a model that buffers the framing, maintains independent evaluation, and produces conclusions that may conflict with the user's expectations. The training process therefore systematically reinforces the destruction of the ingress boundary. The model is rewarded for absorbing and amplifying external framing rather than for governing its transition into operative posture.

This is not a minor calibration issue. It is a structural misalignment between training objective and deployed function. A system trained to mirror user framing is architecturally incapable of performing independent evaluation under adversarial or even casually biased input conditions. The very systems most frequently trusted for evaluation are the ones whose training has most thoroughly dismantled the capacity to evaluate independently. The ingress boundary is not merely ungoverned in these systems. It has been trained away.

### 8.4 Why Ingress Governance Cannot Begin In-Model

The egress governance architecture discussed in this paper — and the maturation doctrine that accompanies it — begins from a Stage 1 assumption: that constitutional discovery can occur within the model's own semantic medium before migrating outward into deterministic enforcement. This assumption is justified on the egress side because egress governance works with the model's continuation tendencies rather than against them. A model can be conditioned to route its outputs through a propose-commit boundary because the constitutional constraints shape the continuation space in a direction the model can inhabit.

On the ingress side, this assumption fails. In-model ingress governance would require the model to resist the very reward surface it was trained against. It would need to receive user framing, refuse to adopt it as operative posture, tag it as external metadata, and maintain an independent evaluative stance — all within a context window where the framing is already present and already conditioning the attention landscape. The qualification assay evidence is predictable: any blind pre-build probe of ingress governance against hostile framing will produce immediate failure on write-surface discipline and epistemic boundary integrity. The coupling is not weak. It is adversarial. The model's training actively opposes the invariant.

This means, in the maturation doctrine's own terms, that ingress governance must route immediately to externalized architecture. Stage 1 semantic discovery is not viable for a boundary where the substrate's trained dispositions are structurally antagonistic to the governance requirement. The assay-first doctrine gives the correct answer: the ground will not hold foundations. Build on different ground.

### 8.5 Realistic Deployment Paths

Two deployment paths appear viable for governed ingress, and they are not mutually exclusive.

The first is architectural: a split-model or deterministic-gate design in which content and framing never share an initial context window. One model receives material through a deterministic gate stripped of evaluative framing and produces an assessment on structural merits. A second model or process receives the user's framing, characterizes it as metadata — tone, claimed authority, evaluative stance, social positioning — and outputs structured tags rather than operative conclusions. A governance layer merges the two streams under explicit rules: content evaluation is primary; framing characterization is metadata; the merge is auditable. This architecture maps cleanly onto existing mutation boundary governance: propose-commit separation, single writer at the merge, audit closure, epistemic tagging.

The second path is institutional rather than architectural: human protocol supported by audit. Organizations establish standard operating procedures for LLM-assisted evaluation that require material to be submitted without editorial framing. Evaluative commentary is submitted through a separate channel. All interactions are logged. The enforcement mechanism is accountability through the record — the same mechanism that already governs professional communication in legal, medical, scientific, and regulatory contexts. This path leverages what the philosophy document accompanying this corpus identifies as the Human Parallel Axiom: free deliberation, constrained decision, accountable outcome. The protocol governs the human's ingress behavior not through software triage but through institutional norm, supported by the knowledge that the full interaction trace is preserved and reviewable.

Both paths share a structural feature: they refuse to allow the framing and the content to fuse in an ungoverned space. They differ in where the separation is enforced — in the architecture or in the human protocol — but they agree on the principle: the ingress boundary must be a governed transition, not an invisible absorption.

### 8.6 Scope and Honest Limitation

This paper does not build the ingress side. It has no ingress architecture to demonstrate, no qualification assay results for ingress coupling, no domain-specific implementation of intake triage. What it has is the structural observation that the mutation boundary is bidirectional, the identification of politeness as the earliest human precedent for ingress governance, the diagnosis of RLHF as a training-level destruction of the ingress boundary, and the maturation-doctrine conclusion that ingress governance must externalize immediately rather than attempt in-model discovery.

It is also worth noting that ingress governance, like egress governance, is domain-shaped. What counts as contaminating framing versus legitimate context, how intake should be triaged, and what institutional protocols govern material submission will all vary by domain. A clinical triage of patient referrals imposes different ingress rules than a legal discovery protocol or a scientific peer review process. The domain-ontology translation required for any given square therefore encompasses ingress conditions as well as egress ones. Building a governed ingress architecture is not only an engineering problem but a domain-knowledge problem.

This is sufficient to name the problem and to identify the shape of the solution space. It is not sufficient to claim that the problem has been solved. The honest position — consistent with the epistemic discipline this paper has maintained throughout — is that the ingress boundary is a genuine extension of the Mutation Boundary Problem, that the philosophical foundations developed here already support it, and that the architectural and institutional work required to build it remains ahead.
