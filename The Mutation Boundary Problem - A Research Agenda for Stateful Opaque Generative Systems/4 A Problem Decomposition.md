## 4. A Problem Decomposition

If the Mutation Boundary Problem is to function as a useful research agenda rather than a rhetorical label, it must be decomposed into tractable domains of inquiry. The problem is too broad to be answered at once, and too composite to be reduced to a single engineering primitive. What follows is therefore not a claim that these domains are exhaustive or final, but a structured attempt to identify the major question clusters that recur whenever opaque generative systems are permitted to persist, change, and participate across time. Each cluster names a pressure point at the mutation boundary. Together they define a research program.

### 4.1 Ontology and Persistent State

The first question cluster concerns what kinds of things a generative system is even allowed to persist. Before governance can regulate mutation, there must be some account of what exists at the durable state surface. This includes not only obvious categories such as memory or goals, but potentially relations, commitments, hypotheses, role state, tool-use context, task posture, or domain-specific records. The research challenge here is not simply to store more information, but to determine what kinds of information must be made addressable if continuity is to survive over time.

Several questions follow. What must persist for a system to count as the “same” participant across cycles? Which kinds of state are identity-bearing, and which are merely situational? How should durable state be represented so that later mutations remain intelligible rather than diffuse? What makes a reference stable enough to survive long horizons: free text, controlled labels, canonical paths, enumerated values, externalized dictionaries, or some hybrid? The answers may vary by domain, but no mutation governance architecture can avoid them. If the state surface is under-specified, then mutation cannot be meaningfully bounded.

Underlying all of these questions is a prior challenge that is rarely named explicitly: domain-ontology translation. Before any of the above can be answered for a particular deployment, the domain expert must translate their field's ontology — what things exist and have durable consequence in this domain, how they are structured, what transitions are permitted, what counts as evidence for mutation — into the canonical address space that governance will operate over. This is not a mechanical task. It requires domain expertise that cannot be automated away. A clinical governance system cannot share its ontology with a narrative character system or a legal proceedings system, even though all three may implement the same OTTC rectangle. Each domain is a constitutional world: a structured ensemble of durable things, lawful transitions, evidentiary standards, and authority assignments that define what legitimate mutation looks like within that world. The ontology question is therefore the first question not only because it is logically prior to continuity, epistemic boundaries, or governance mechanics, but because it is the act of translation through which a domain expert brings their field into the address space of a governed architecture. Without it, governance has no substance.

### 4.2 Continuity and Identity

Once a persistent state surface exists, the next question is what continuity consists in. For opaque generative systems, continuity cannot simply be equated with style consistency or retrieval quality. A system may sound similar across turns while quietly changing its commitments, beliefs, or self-relations in unstable ways. Conversely, a system may legitimately change while remaining recognizably continuous. This raises a central problem: how should continuity be defined for an entity whose internal process is probabilistic and partially hidden, but whose external participation must remain intelligible?

Research in this cluster asks what makes change lawful rather than drift. Which dimensions of state may shift quickly, and which should be stability-biased? What counts as an earned change, and what counts as arbitrary self-rewrite? How should thresholds, step limits, override conditions, or special evidence operate? Can continuity be preserved through invariant-preserving transitions rather than through claims about inner coherence? These questions are especially visible in long-horizon and role-based systems, but they apply anywhere persistent identity matters. The point is not to freeze a system, but to make change legible enough that continuity survives it.

### 4.3 Perspective and Epistemic Boundaries

A third cluster concerns bounded perspective. Generative systems, especially language models, often collapse uncertainty into fluent assertion unless constrained otherwise. Yet meaningful participation in a shared world requires more than correct content; it requires lawful epistemic position. A participant may perceive, infer, speculate, intend, request evidence, or report memory, but these are not equivalent acts. If such distinctions are not structurally represented, then the system will tend to flatten them into a single undifferentiated discourse surface, where hypotheses harden into facts and hidden states become casually knowable.

The research questions here concern how point of view should be modeled and enforced. What may a system claim as directly known? What must be labeled as inference, uncertainty, or expectation? How should epistemic violations be defined in different domains? What forms of humility are performative only, and what forms are constitutionally meaningful? How can a system remain compelling or useful while remaining explicitly bounded? These questions are central not only in narrative domains, where omniscience quickly breaks plausibility, but also in analytic, scientific, medical, and enterprise settings where unsupported certainty can be materially harmful. The mutation boundary is weak wherever epistemic status can mutate silently into apparent fact.

### 4.4 Mutation and Governance

This cluster sits at the center of the paper’s argument. If state exists and continuity matters, then there must be some mechanism for determining how state changes occur. The core distinction here is between exploration and commitment. Generative systems are valuable in part because they can entertain possibilities, weigh alternatives, and produce interpretive variation. But if exploratory output is allowed to write directly into durable state, then the result is often drift, thrash, or arbitrary self-modification. Mutation governance therefore concerns the design of the boundary itself: how proposals are formed, what operations are legal, who or what may authorize a commit, and how conflicts are resolved.

The research questions are both architectural and conceptual. What is the minimal lawful operation set for durable mutation? How should proposal and commitment be separated? Should there be one commit authority, and if so, where should it reside? How should conflicts between competing proposals be reconciled without collapsing into arbitrary fiat? What forms of direct write must be prohibited? Under what conditions should certain mutations be vetoed, downgraded, capped, or suppressed? This cluster is the most obviously procedural, but it is inseparable from deeper questions of legitimacy and intelligibility. Governance is not merely an implementation detail; it is the institutional form of the mutation boundary.

### 4.5 Memory, History, and Causality

Persistent participation requires some relation to the past, but the past cannot simply accumulate without structure. Memory in long-horizon generative systems is not only a storage problem. It is also a problem of causality, salience, revision, and consequence. What gets remembered, what gets forgotten, what may be updated retroactively, how memories link to later changes, and how much of the past remains active are all decisions that shape the legibility of state over time. If memory is treated as ungoverned residue, continuity becomes clutter rather than history.

The key research questions in this cluster include: when should experiences crystallize into persistent records? What thresholds of salience, consequence, repetition, or affect justify admission? How should memories link to changed fields, entities, goals, or hypotheses so that later state remains interpretable? What forms of decay or reconsolidation are lawful? How should past events influence current state without either freezing the system in trauma or dissolving all historical consequence into recency effects? The broader issue is how to preserve causal traceability across time. Without such traceability, mutation may be visible but not meaningful.

### 4.6 Error, Failure, and Anti-Fragility

A durable participant in a shared world will not be perfect. The question is therefore not whether errors occur, but what kinds of errors matter, how they are classified, and whether they destroy the system’s value or become interpretable within it. This cluster concerns the taxonomy of failure. Some domains treat any incorrectness as unacceptable. Others can absorb certain deviations as informative, reparable, or even value-bearing if they remain legible. This is especially important for narrative domains, where a coherent error may become part of character or plot rather than pure system breakdown. But even outside narrative, not all deviations are equal.

Research here asks what counts as failure at the mutation boundary. Is the failure an unsupported claim, an unlawful state write, an invisible drift, an audit inconsistency, a violation of epistemic scope, or something else? When does an error become catastrophic, and when can it remain bounded and interpretable? Can a system be anti-fragile in the sense that certain governed deviations generate useful structure rather than only collapse? Under what domains, risks, and constraints is this tolerable? These questions matter because mutation governance must distinguish between variability that preserves meaningful participation and instability that destroys it.

### 4.7 Tools, Evidence, and Numeric Authority

As soon as generative systems are permitted to use tools, query databases, call calculators, search the web, or actuate external systems, the mutation boundary extends beyond internal state. Tool use creates a bridge between semantic interpretation and external evidence or action. Yet many architectures still treat tool invocation as a local tactical choice rather than as a governed transition. This is insufficient. A tool call is also an epistemic act: it expresses a need for evidence, a claim of relevance, and sometimes a request for world-changing authority.

The research challenge here includes at least three parts. First, when should a system be required to request evidence or computation rather than infer freely? Second, how should tool requests be governed, logged, and authorized? Third, how should externally returned values re-enter the semantic system? These questions connect directly to what some architectures may call a semantic–numeric split: the system reasons in canonical semantic categories while precise calculation, thresholding, and comparison occur in deterministic machinery. The boundary between meaning and numeric authority remains poorly understood in current practice, yet it is central wherever exact values matter. A system that improvises numbers, or treats retrieved values as ungoverned narrative material, remains weak at the mutation boundary even if its tool stack is sophisticated.

### 4.8 Coupling, Assay, and Substrate Fit

A further research cluster concerns a problem often ignored in formal architecture design: the lawful interface must fit the substrate that inhabits it. The parallel with interpretability is instructive here: just as a complete causal account of why a system produced a given output does not by itself constitute the rules under which that output may become durable state, a well-formed schema or machine-readable interface does not by itself constitute the governance that makes structured outputs consequential. In both cases, diagnostic access to a layer of the system — whether interior reasoning or exterior form — is a necessary but insufficient condition for governance. Opaque generative systems do not interact with every schema or DSL equally well. Some distinctions carry enough semantic weight to remain stable; others collapse into shallow formatting or brittle imitation. This means that mutation governance cannot be designed as though any formal structure can simply be imposed from above. The system must be qualified, tested, or assayed to determine what kinds of canonical references, instruction patterns, and update laws it can actually hold.

This raises several important questions. How do we distinguish parseability from real coupling? What kinds of prompt or schema failures reveal weak constitutional grip? How do language, culture, training priors, and reinforcement patterns alter the shape of the workable law surface? Why might one model sustain a particular ontology or operation vocabulary while another only mimics it superficially? How should architecture differ when the system is only fit to propose versus fit to verify or commit? This cluster is methodological as much as architectural. It concerns how one learns what kind of governance a given substrate can bear before building too much on unstable assumptions.

### 4.9 Maturation and Externalization

Even if a workable mutation protocol can be discovered in-model, another cluster concerns how and when that protocol should migrate outward into more deterministic forms. Long-horizon, high-risk, or multi-agent systems cannot rely indefinitely on in-context semantics alone. Yet complete externalization is often impossible or premature before the semantic distinctions themselves have been discovered and stabilized. This suggests a maturation problem: how governance moves from exploratory language-native form to externalized enforcement without losing the distinctions that made it workable in the first place.

Research questions here include: what must first be discovered inside the generative substrate before it can be safely externalized? Which components are most natural to externalize early: storage, canonical dictionaries, validators, commit authority, verification, modifier expiry, tool authorization, numeric analytics? How should migration be staged? What are the risks of externalizing too late versus too early? What signals indicate that a system has outgrown a purely in-context governance layer? This cluster matters because the mutation boundary is not merely a static design problem. It is also a developmental one.

### 4.10 Multi-Agent and Institutional Form

The mutation boundary becomes more complex when multiple generative systems share or negotiate over the same world. A single agent with weak governance can drift; multiple agents with overlapping state and no shared constitutional order can dissolve shared reality much faster. This introduces questions of institutional form. How should canonical address spaces be shared? What happens when different generators possess only partial views of a larger ontology? How should overlapping jurisdictions be managed? When does a shared dictionary or external state authority become necessary?

This cluster asks what the minimal political form is for multi-agent stochastic participation. Must there be one writer and many proposers? Can different domains have separate governors while sharing a common token dictionary? How should inter-agent conflict be reconciled? How should ontologies evolve when many participants depend on them? These questions extend the mutation boundary from an internal governance issue to an inter-agent constitutional one. They matter not only for complex software agents, but for any future environment in which multiple opaque generators are expected to co-inhabit durable structures.

### 4.11 Domain-Specific Success Criteria

Finally, no mutation-boundary architecture can be evaluated without domain-specific criteria for success. Different domains value different things. A medical setting may prioritize factual grounding, suppression of unauthorized action, and audit completeness. An enterprise setting may prioritize approval flow, replayability, and policy compliance. A scientific setting may prioritize explicit hypotheses, evidence handling, and falsification structure. A narrative setting may prioritize continuity, bounded subjectivity, emotional credibility, and meaningful change. Without domain-sensitive criteria, architectures risk optimizing for the wrong form of order.

The key questions here are therefore evaluative and normative. What counts as success in a given domain? Which kinds of error are tolerable, and which are not? Is the primary metric correctness, coherence, control, interpretability, or emotional force? How should comparative improvement be measured? To what degree can a system remain useful if it is legible but imperfect? These questions cannot all be settled in the abstract. But they must be stated explicitly, because a mutation-boundary architecture that succeeds in one domain may be inadequate or dangerous in another.

Taken together, these eleven clusters define the broad terrain of the Mutation Boundary Problem. They are not offered as final categories, nor do they imply that every future system must implement the same response to each. Their value is organizational. They make visible the fact that long-horizon participation by opaque generative systems is not a single engineering challenge but a layered problem spanning representation, continuity, epistemics, authority, memory, evidence, coupling, institutional design, and evaluation.
