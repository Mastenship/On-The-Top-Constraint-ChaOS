## 3. Why Current Framings Are Insufficient

The rapid development of large language models and related generative systems has produced several dominant framings for thinking about their risks and capabilities. Among the most common are capability, alignment, memory, tool use, prompting, orchestration, and evaluation. Each of these framings captures a genuine part of the design space. Each has generated useful methods and important insights. Yet none, either alone or in loose combination, fully captures the problem introduced in the previous section. The Mutation Boundary Problem remains under-articulated because most prevailing framings are oriented toward outputs, tasks, or policies in isolation rather than toward the governance of durable change across time.

A capability framing asks what the system can do. Can it solve tasks, reason over documents, call tools, plan multi-step actions, or maintain consistency under load? Such questions matter, especially when generative systems are increasingly embedded in workflows and decision environments. But capability-centered framings tend to measure momentary performance rather than the conditions under which performance becomes durable participation. A system may be highly capable in task completion and still be weakly governed at the point where its outputs become persistent records, new commitments, altered state, or actionable beliefs. Capability therefore does not answer the question of whether the system's changes remain lawful, interpretable, or accountable over time.

An alignment framing asks whether the system behaves in ways consistent with human goals, policies, or values. This too is indispensable. However, alignment is often treated as if it were primarily a matter of output content, instruction following, refusal behavior, or value adherence. These are important aspects of system behavior, but they do not by themselves specify a durable mutation protocol. A system can appear aligned in local outputs while still altering its persistent state in opaque, unstable, or poorly justified ways. Conversely, some mutation-boundary problems arise even when no obvious alignment breach is present: for example, when a transient stressor is silently converted into a standing commitment, or when speculation is reified into record. Alignment asks whether the system behaves properly; the mutation boundary problem asks under what rules behavior is allowed to become enduring structure.

A memory framing asks how systems can retain information across interactions. Current work often emphasizes retrieval quality, context management, episodic storage, summarization, or long-context persistence. These are necessary components of any long-horizon architecture. But memory alone does not distinguish between lawful continuity and uncontrolled accumulation. What gets remembered, what gets revised, what is treated as authoritative, how prior content is allowed to update later state, and which parts of the past may be overridden are governance questions, not merely storage questions. A system that "has memory" may still drift badly if the memory surface is uncontrolled or if retrieval is allowed to masquerade as justified mutation. In this sense, memory is one substrate of the mutation boundary problem, but not its solution.

A tool-use framing asks how generative systems can interact with external resources such as search, APIs, calculators, databases, sensors, or actuators. This framing is especially important because tool use often marks the point where a stochastic system touches the external world. Yet tool-use research is frequently structured around selection accuracy, action chaining, execution success, or safety gating in narrow terms. These concerns matter, but they do not exhaust the deeper issue. A tool call is not merely an action; it is also a claim about evidentiary need, legitimacy, authority, and consequence. If a model can call a tool impulsively, treat the result uncritically, or fold externally retrieved information into durable state without a lawful proposal-and-commit process, then the mutation boundary remains weak even if the tool pipeline itself is technically functional. Tool use therefore sharpens the problem; it does not replace it.

A prompting or orchestration framing asks how output can be shaped through instructions, workflow patterns, role splits, planners, validators, and process decomposition. This is one of the most fertile practical areas in contemporary LLM engineering. Many useful systems already rely on prompt protocols, intermediate artifacts, multi-pass structures, or supervisory chains. However, these framings often remain procedural without becoming constitutional. They tell us how to produce better outputs, but less often how to distinguish exploratory reasoning from durable mutation, or how to ensure that persistent state is changed only through lawful operations with accountable authority. In other words, orchestration can improve the path to an output without necessarily resolving who is allowed to change the world that future outputs inherit.

An evaluation framing asks how systems should be measured. Common metrics include accuracy, robustness, bias, hallucination rate, helpfulness, task success, calibration, and safety failures. These are all useful and often necessary metrics. Yet most evaluation still centers on outputs, tasks, or benchmark scenarios rather than on mutation quality over long horizons. A system may score well on conventional benchmarks while remaining structurally weak at continuity, state legibility, or lawful change. If no metric captures the difference between proposal and commitment, between transient and durable state, or between visible history and accountable mutation, then significant forms of failure may remain invisible to standard evaluation regimes. This is one reason the mutation boundary problem requires its own metrics rather than relying only on existing ones.

A related insufficiency lies in the dominance of what might be called the transcript view of generative systems. In many current deployments, the transcript is treated as the primary artifact: the main object of meaning, audit, and evaluation. But transcripts conflate too much. They mix exploration, interpretation, justification, style, and sometimes consequence into one stream. They often obscure the distinction between what was considered, what was proposed, what was believed, what was committed, and what was actually changed. For stateless or low-stakes interactions this may be tolerable. For long-horizon systems with persistent participation, it becomes increasingly problematic. A transcript may tell us what was said while hiding the rules, or absence of rules, by which saying became state.

Another insufficiency is the frequent assumption that more memory, more context, or more tooling will automatically solve long-horizon coherence. In practice, these additions can intensify the underlying problem if mutation remains poorly governed. More memory can preserve more contradictions. More context can preserve more ambiguity about what counts as authoritative. More tools can introduce more opportunities for premature fact-hardening or unjustified action. More orchestration can create more moving parts without clarifying where commitment authority actually resides. Without an explicit account of the mutation boundary, scaling surrounding capabilities may simply increase the volume of illegible change.

Current framings also tend to underplay the problem of bounded perspective. This is especially visible in roleplay and narrative settings but generalizes far beyond them. A system that can produce fluent language will often overstep its epistemic position unless constrained otherwise. It may speak as if outcomes are already known, as if hidden states of others are directly accessible, or as if internal confidence is equivalent to evidence. Traditional hallucination framings capture only part of this. The broader issue is that a participant in a shared world must not only avoid false statements; it must occupy a lawful point of view. Most current architectures do not make that distinction operational enough, and as a result they fail to preserve subjective legitimacy even when they remain locally coherent.

Finally, prevailing framings often assume that if a system can be made parseable, then it has become governable. This is a major conceptual mistake. A parser can extract structure from unstable behavior. A schema can be imposed on text that the model does not actually inhabit robustly. A well-formed JSON object can still carry semantically weak or drifting commitments. The fact that something can be extracted, normalized, or logged does not mean that the system has been constitutionally coupled to a stable law surface. This is one reason later sections will emphasize assay, substrate fit, and the difference between parseability and coupling. Mutation governance depends not only on what can be extracted after the fact, but on what distinctions the system can reliably inhabit before durable mutation occurs.

For all these reasons, existing framings should not be discarded but repositioned. The Mutation Boundary Problem does not supplant the work being done under capability, alignment, memory, tool use, orchestration, or evaluation. It reveals what that work is missing and, in doing so, clarifies what each framing becomes when situated relative to the boundary it has not yet named.

The claim is one of holistic synthesis, not replacement. Each existing research program addresses a genuine dimension of the problem. But without an explicit account of the mutation boundary, those dimensions remain disconnected—each solving part of the challenge without a shared reference frame for understanding how their contributions compose into governed participation over time. The Mutation Boundary Problem provides that reference frame. Once it is foregrounded, the significance of adjacent work does not diminish. It is relocated and, in many cases, sharpened.

Consider what each framing becomes under this relocation. Capability research reveals what a system can do, but the mutation boundary asks under what authority doing becomes durable change. Capability is therefore not diminished; it becomes the study of what a governed system must be able to perform in order to inhabit the boundary lawfully. Without capability, there is nothing to govern. Without boundary governance, capability produces unaccountable force.

Alignment research orients the interior dispositions of a system toward goals, values, or policies consistent with human intent. This remains indispensable—because a system whose internal tendencies are well-aligned is more likely to respect the mutation boundary rather than strain against it. But alignment without boundary governance is trust without protocol: it asks the interior to behave well without specifying the constitutional form through which good behavior becomes verifiable at the point of durable consequence. Boundary governance without alignment, conversely, is protocol inhabited by an indifferent or adversarial interior. The two are complementary, not competing. Together they address both disposition and transition; apart, each leaves half the problem unresolved.

Memory research engineers the substrates of persistence—what is stored, retrieved, summarized, and carried forward. Under the mutation boundary framing, this work becomes the construction of the durable state surface itself. Its significance is not reduced but refined: the central questions shift from whether the system can retrieve more to under what lawful conditions retrieval results are allowed to become committed state. Memory research builds the terrain on which governance operates; the mutation boundary specifies the rules of lawful passage across that terrain.

Tool-use research governs the interface between stochastic generation and external consequence. Under the mutation boundary framing, tool calls are recognized not merely as actions but as claims about evidentiary need, authority, and consequence. Tool-use research becomes the study of how the mutation boundary extends beyond internal state into the world. Its importance is amplified, not diminished, because every tool invocation is a potential site of ungoverned transition.

Orchestration and prompting research shapes the procedural path from input to output. Under the mutation boundary framing, orchestration becomes the study of how exploratory processes are structured so that the distinction between proposal and commitment can be maintained throughout complex workflows. The contribution is real and necessary; what changes is that orchestration is no longer treated as sufficient in itself but as one component of a broader constitutional architecture.

Evaluation research asks how systems should be measured. Under the mutation boundary framing, current evaluation methods retain their value for local assessment but are recognized as structurally unable to detect certain classes of failure. Specifically, short-horizon evaluation cannot distinguish between a system that is deeply governed and one that is merely locally compliant—just as, in the language of the equivalence principle, local measurement cannot distinguish between reference frames that diverge only over extended observation. Long-horizon, multi-transition, adversarial-pressure evaluation becomes not merely a practical improvement but a theoretical necessity: it is where the adequacy of a governance architecture is genuinely tested.

Interpretability research, though not always listed among the dominant framings above, deserves explicit mention here. Under the mutation boundary framing, interpretability does not become the path to governance—governance operates at the boundary, not the interior. But interpretability becomes the study of what makes a generator capable of inhabiting governance well. A system with more coherent internal representations, more stable reasoning structures, more faithful intermediate states, is a system more likely to sustain lawful participation over long horizons without producing the kinds of tidal-force failures that reveal shallow compliance. Interpretability builds better inhabitants for the constitutional form, even though it does not replace that form.

The point of this relocation is not diplomatic. It is structural. A body of research that addresses capability, alignment, memory, tools, orchestration, evaluation, and interpretability in isolation—without a shared account of where and how exploratory generation becomes durable consequence—will continue to produce locally excellent work that composes poorly. Each framing solves its own problem. The Mutation Boundary Problem names the problem that emerges between them: the governance of the transition itself. What is needed is not the victory of one framing over others, but a holistic synthesis in which each finds its place relative to the boundary that connects them all.
