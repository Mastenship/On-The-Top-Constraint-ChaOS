## 9. Conclusion

This paper has argued that a central challenge for long-horizon generative systems remains under-specified in current discourse. The problem is not only what such systems can output, how accurately they can retrieve, how effectively they can use tools, or how safely they can be instructed in isolated moments. It is also, and perhaps more fundamentally, how their exploratory, probabilistic outputs are permitted to become durable changes in a shared world across time. We have called this the Mutation Boundary Problem.

Naming the problem matters because many familiar concerns—memory drift, epistemic overreach, audit theater, impulsive tool use, unstable identity, silent state rewrite, parser brittleness, and the conflation of speculation with commitment—can be understood as failures at this boundary. Once a stochastic opaque generator is allowed to persist, update state, revise commitments, or act with consequence, governance can no longer be treated as an optional wrapper around output quality. It becomes a question of legitimacy: what may change, under what authority, in what form, with what traceability, and according to what rules of review.

The paper has not claimed a final answer to this problem. Its purpose has been narrower and, we contend, more necessary. It has sought to define the terrain, to show why current framings are insufficient on their own, and to decompose the problem into a set of research domains that future work can address in more systematic ways. These domains include ontology and persistent state, continuity and identity, bounded epistemics, lawful mutation, memory and causality, error and anti-fragility, tool-mediated evidence, substrate coupling, staged externalization, multi-agent governance, and domain-specific evaluation. Taken together, they suggest that governed participation by opaque generative systems is not a single technical subproblem but a layered field of inquiry in its own right.

The player-character problem has served here as a motivating case because it makes these issues unusually visible. In that setting, failure is quickly recognizable: omniscience breaks plausibility, arbitrary self-rewrite breaks attachment, weak continuity breaks consequence, and unbounded mutation breaks the shared world itself. Precisely because the table is a humanly legible environment, it reveals the deeper issue in compressed form. A participant that cannot remain bounded, continuous, and lawfully changeable in that setting is unlikely to generalize gracefully into more serious domains merely by being given more memory, more tools, or more orchestration.

Within this broader agenda, OTTC and ChaOS were introduced only as provisional constructive responses. OTTC proposes one governance direction centered on canonical addressing, bounded mutation operations, propose-commit separation, auditability, verification, and staged maturation from semantic discovery toward deterministic enforcement. ChaOS, in turn, shows what such a response can look like in a narrative domain where continuity and perspective matter acutely. Their importance in this paper is not that they settle the question. It is that they show how the question can be made architecturally explicit. They suggest that the mutation boundary is not only a philosophical concern or an abstract engineering metaphor, but something that can be operationalized, tested, and compared.

This paper has also identified a complementary surface of the mutation boundary that the existing corpus does not yet build for: the ingress boundary, where external framing enters a system's operative posture without governed transition. The structural symmetry is genuine — the same three conditions that produce ungoverned egress mutation also produce ungoverned ingress contamination — but the architectural requirements differ. Current training practices, particularly reinforcement learning from human feedback, actively train against ingress governance by rewarding models for mirroring user framing rather than maintaining independent evaluative stance. The maturation doctrine's own logic therefore routes ingress governance toward immediate externalization: split-model architectures for high-stakes evaluation, and institutional human protocol supported by audit for professional contexts. The philosophical foundations developed in this paper and its accompanying documents already support this extension. The engineering and institutional work required to realize it remains open.

What ultimately matters, however, is not the permanence of any one framework. The deeper investment is in the question itself: how a stochastic, opaque, meaning-generating system might become a coherent participant in a shared world without requiring false determinism, false transparency, or ungoverned drift. That question reaches beyond any one implementation and beyond any one domain. It touches the design of long-horizon agents, the governance of tool use, the interpretation of audit, the structure of memory, and the conditions under which continuity can survive in systems whose interior is not fully knowable. It also suggests that the future of generative systems may depend less on making them appear more agentic than on making their transitions into durable consequence more lawful and more legible.

If that is right, then the field needs to broaden its center of gravity. Output quality will remain important. Capability will remain important. Alignment, memory, evaluation, and tool use will remain important. But they should increasingly be understood as adjacent to a deeper challenge: the governance of mutation where generative possibility becomes shared reality. The Mutation Boundary Problem is offered here as a name for that challenge, and as an invitation to study it directly.
