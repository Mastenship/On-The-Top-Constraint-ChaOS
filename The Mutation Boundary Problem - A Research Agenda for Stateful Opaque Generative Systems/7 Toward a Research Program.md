## 7. Toward a Research Program

If the Mutation Boundary Problem is to become more than a descriptive insight, it must be turned into a comparative and testable research program. That requires moving beyond general intuitions about governance, continuity, or legibility and asking what can actually be studied across architectures, domains, languages, and model classes. The purpose of this section is therefore not to outline one canonical experimental design, but to outline the kinds of empirical and conceptual work needed to make progress. The broad claim is simple: if stateful opaque generative systems are to be trusted as participants in long-horizon settings, then evaluation must shift from output quality alone toward the governance quality of durable change.

A first priority is to distinguish unit qualification from system regression. Many current workflows test only the performance of a built system and then infer backwards that the underlying model is fit for the role it has been assigned. OTTC’s assay-first doctrine suggests a different discipline that is worth investigating more broadly. Before a machine or ontology is built around a candidate model, the model should be probed in a fresh and minimally conditioned setting to determine whether it can reliably inhabit the intended constitutional interface at all. This includes, at minimum, testing canonical address obedience, write-surface discipline, epistemic boundary integrity where applicable, and audit non-theater. Such qualification results should then inform role assignment, authority scope, and externalization depth. Post-build retesting remains necessary, but it answers a different question: whether the engineered system has preserved or degraded invariants after design choices were made. Research that conflates these two stages risks misclassifying conditioned compliance as underlying coupling strength.

A second priority is the development of mutation-boundary metrics. Standard evaluations of generative systems often focus on accuracy, task completion, latency, safety refusals, or user satisfaction. These are useful but insufficient for the present problem. Progress here requires metrics that capture whether durable state changes remain lawful and interpretable over time. Candidate measures include canonical address compliance, rate of illegal direct-write attempts, audit coverage correctness, state-thrash frequency, epistemic boundary violation rate, verification coherence, and replayability of state deltas under repeated scenario batteries. Domain-specific additions will also be needed. Narrative systems may require blinded human ratings of continuity, bounded subjectivity, and player-likeness. Scientific or analytic systems may require rates of unsupported hypothesis hardening, evidence-request discipline, or provenance completeness. The key point is that mutation quality must become measurable as its own object.

A third research direction concerns comparative constitutional architectures. OTTC is one proposed response to the mutation boundary, but it should not be treated as the only candidate family. The field would benefit from systematic comparisons among systems with different governance shapes: free-form memory architectures, planner-validator pipelines, retrieval-heavy agents, schema-first deterministic wrappers, and propose-commit constitutional systems. Such comparisons should ask not only which architecture performs better on local tasks, but which maintains more legible and bounded state evolution over long horizons. In this respect, the Mutation Boundary Problem may help reframe comparison itself. Rather than asking only which system is “smarter,” the field can ask which system remains more governable as persistence, consequence, and time increase.

A fourth priority is language-conditioned coupling research. Because governance in these systems is partly language-native, coupling strength should not be assumed to transfer cleanly across languages, cultures, or rhetorical surfaces. The same constitution may hold strongly in one language and weakly in another. Canonical token discipline, epistemic modesty, deference to operation vocabularies, and tolerance for ambiguity may all vary across model family and operating language. This has important implications for multilingual or globally deployed systems. Research should therefore investigate not only whether a constitutional interface works, but where and under what language conditions it works. Such work may influence canonical lexicon design, translation-before-commit policies, and the placement of deterministic boundaries in multilingual settings.

A fifth line of work concerns the relation between semantic governance and deterministic enforcement. One of the central claims emerging from OTTC is that semantic distinctions must often be discovered in-model before they can be safely externalized. But this claim itself should be tested, refined, and perhaps bounded. Under what conditions can a schema-first design succeed without assay-first discovery? What kinds of semantic distinctions survive externalization cleanly, and which ones collapse when moved too early into rigid software? How much of a governance protocol must be inhabited by the model itself before deterministic systems can meaningfully enforce it? Conversely, at what point does remaining too long in pure in-model governance accumulate unacceptable risk? These questions are architectural and developmental rather than purely performance-based, and they are likely to differ by domain and consequence level.

Related to this is the need for research on maturation pathways. OTTC proposes a Stage 1 / Stage 2 / Stage 3 developmental doctrine: semantic discovery, hybrid migration, deterministic enforcement. This sequence is plausible, but it should be treated as an empirical claim about engineering practice rather than a self-validating narrative. Comparative studies could examine systems that begin with fully externalized state, systems that remain in-context, and systems that migrate gradually. Relevant outcomes might include parser brittleness, schema drift, authority confusion, governance debt, and cost of redesign after failure. Such work would help determine whether staged maturation is broadly necessary, conditionally valuable, or domain-specific.

A sixth research area is tool mediation as evidence governance. Once tools enter the picture, the mutation boundary no longer concerns only internal state. It also concerns requests for information, calculations, API calls, and real-world actions. The field needs a better understanding of when a system should be allowed to infer, when it should be required to request evidence, and how tool requests should themselves be represented and governed. One promising framing is to treat tool calls not merely as execution actions but as auditable evidence motions: proposals that ask a governance layer to authorize a measurement, retrieval, or actuation. Research here could compare direct tool-calling agents against mediated architectures that separate proposal, authorization, execution, and reintegration. Relevant metrics might include overuse, underuse, provenance quality, calibration shift after retrieval, and the impact of external evidence on later state mutation.

A seventh priority is error taxonomy and tolerance research. The paper has argued that different domains assign different meanings to error, and that some domains may tolerate or even productively integrate certain legible deviations while others may not. This suggests the need for a more explicit typology. Which errors are purely constitutional—for example, illegal writes, audit omissions, or verification incoherence? Which are epistemic—for example, unsupported certainty or scope violations? Which are domain-relative—for example, scientifically unacceptable but narratively usable distortions? Under what conditions can governed error remain value-bearing rather than destructive? These questions matter because architectures should not be judged by a single undifferentiated standard of correctness where domain conditions differ fundamentally.

An eighth research direction concerns multi-agent constitutional form. Many future systems will not consist of a single generator acting alone. They will involve multiple models, humans, tools, and external processes sharing some form of state or consequence space. This raises questions about how constitutional order should scale. Can multiple proposing agents safely share one commit authority? When do overlapping ontologies require a common canonical dictionary? How should authority be partitioned across citizen, governor, and auditor roles? What forms of conflict resolution remain intelligible when more than one generator contributes to a shared world? Research here may benefit from drawing not only on computer science and AI safety, but also on organizational theory, institutional design, and jurisprudence, since the problem becomes increasingly political as shared state becomes more socially consequential.

A ninth and especially important line of inquiry concerns domain-specific success metrics. One of the major risks in this area is evaluating all systems as though they serve the same end. They do not. A narrative character system is not successful for the same reasons a scientific analysis assistant is successful. An enterprise workflow governor is not successful for the same reasons a roleplay participant is successful. The Mutation Boundary Problem does not erase these differences; it provides a way to compare them without collapsing them. Future work should therefore be explicit about what counts as success in each domain. For narrative systems, this may include continuity, emotional legibility, bounded point of view, and meaningful transformation. For analytical systems, it may include evidence discipline, provenance quality, and explicit uncertainty handling. For high-stakes domains, it may include suppression of unauthorized mutation and the reliability of deterministic oversight. Without such explicitness, governance architectures risk being overgeneralized or misapplied.

A tenth research direction — domain-expert constitutional discovery — deserves explicit identification. The analysis in this paper implies that any new OTTC-governed architecture requires prior ontology translation: the domain expert must render their field's constitutional world (durable things, lawful transitions, evidentiary standards, authority assignments) into OTTC-legible form before governance can operate. But the method by which this translation happens is itself understudied. This paper's corpus is designed to function as a machine-facing collaboration scaffold: when loaded into persistent LLM context, it allows LLMs to assist domain experts in articulating ontology surfaces, proposing canonical addresses, identifying gaps in lawful mutation coverage, and validating architectural proposals against the rectangle invariants. The domain expert retains commit authority; the LLM proposes; disagreements between model proposals or between model and expert serve as diagnostic signals rather than obstacles. Research questions here include: what makes a domain's ontology tractable for OTTC translation? What forms of collaboration are most productive? What failure modes arise when domain knowledge is insufficiently translated before governance architecture is built? How should the discovery process be scaffolded across different domain types? These questions matter because OTTC's reach as a governance pattern depends directly on how well domain experts can build new squares, and that in turn depends on the quality of the translation and discovery method.

Finally, the research program must remain open to the possibility that current proposals are incomplete, local, or wrong in ways not yet visible. The Mutation Boundary Problem itself is an attempt to name a space of questions more clearly, not to declare those questions closed. This means the field should actively seek falsification conditions. Under what circumstances does a propose-commit architecture fail to improve legibility? When does canonicalization become theater rather than coupling? When does audit collapse into performative compliance? When does a constitutional interface overfit one model family and fail to generalize? When does a domain’s success criterion reveal that a given governance shape is solving the wrong problem? These are not weaknesses in the research agenda; they are signs that the agenda is treating itself seriously.

The immediate value of this program is practical as well as conceptual. By shifting attention from output alone to the quality of governed state transition, it offers a way to compare systems that might otherwise seem incomparable. It suggests new metrics, new assay practices, new architectural questions, and new distinctions between semantic and deterministic authority. It also opens a path for domains that are currently poorly served by standard evaluation regimes, including narrative participation, long-horizon collaborative agents, and other systems in which continuity matters as much as local correctness. The next and final section returns to the central claim of the paper: if opaque generative systems are to matter across time, then the mutation boundary must become a first-class object of design, evaluation, and governance.
