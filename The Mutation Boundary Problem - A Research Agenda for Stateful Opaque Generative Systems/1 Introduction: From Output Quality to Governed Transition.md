# The Mutation Boundary Problem - A Research Agenda for Stateful Opaque Generative Systems

## 1. Introduction: From Output Quality to Governed Transition

Large language models and related generative systems are often evaluated in terms of what they can produce in the moment: whether an answer is useful, whether a tool call is valid, whether a response appears safe, whether a memory mechanism retrieves the right detail, or whether an agent completes a task. These concerns are important, but they do not fully capture a deeper challenge that emerges once such systems are permitted to persist across time, accumulate state, revise internal representations, and participate in ongoing shared contexts. Under those conditions, the central problem is no longer only what the system can generate, but how generation is allowed to become durable consequence.

This becomes especially pressing for stateful opaque generative systems: systems whose internal processes are not fully inspectable, whose outputs are probabilistic rather than deterministic, and whose behavior may nonetheless alter persistent records, commitments, plans, relations, or other forms of shared reality. Such systems do not fail only through incorrect answers or unsafe actions. They also fail when state changes become illegible, when identity drifts without justification, when temporary pressures silently rewrite durable baselines, when hypotheses harden into facts without evidentiary warrant, or when fluent output masks the absence of accountable transition logic. In these cases, the problem is not simply one of error. It is one of governance.

This paper names that challenge the Mutation Boundary Problem. The term refers to the problem of governing the boundary between generative exploration and durable state change in stateful opaque generative systems. Put differently, it concerns the conditions under which a stochastic, language-mediated generator can participate coherently in a shared world across time without requiring false claims of full interior transparency, false claims of determinism, or ungoverned drift into illegible state. The issue is not merely whether such a system can produce plausible outputs. It is whether the transition from “possible continuation” to “committed consequence” can be made intelligible, bounded, and accountable.

Current discourse only partially addresses this problem. Work on alignment asks how systems can be made safer or more aligned with human intent. Work on memory asks how information can be retained across turns. Work on tool use asks how models can retrieve information or act on the world. Work on prompting and orchestration asks how outputs can be shaped and workflows stabilized. Work on evaluation asks how to measure task performance, bias, hallucination, or robustness. Each of these lines of inquiry is valuable. Yet none of them, taken alone, fully answers a more basic question: what must be true for a stochastic opaque generator to persist, change, and act within an ongoing world without rendering that world progressively less legible?

A further insufficiency threads through all of these framings: they generally underspecify the role of domain ontology as a precondition of governance. Before a generative system can participate lawfully in a consequence-bearing domain — clinical records, legal proceedings, financial operations, narrative continuity, scientific inquiry — its state surface must be constituted through that domain's own distinctions: what things exist and are mutable, what counts as evidence, what transitions are lawful, who holds authority, how conflicts resolve. Without that prior act of ontology translation, governance architecture provides structure without substance. Governance cannot regulate what it cannot address, and what must be made addressable is always domain-specific. Domains are not mere vocabularies; they are constitutional worlds. Each imposes its own law surface on the mutation boundary.

This paper argues that the field needs a more explicit research program around that question. The aim here is not to present a final theory of governed stochastic participation, nor to claim that any existing framework has already solved the problem. Rather, the goal is to formalize the problem domain itself, show why it matters, and decompose it into tractable lines of inquiry. The paper therefore proceeds as a research agenda. It identifies the major conceptual and engineering questions that arise once generative systems are treated not merely as output devices, but as participants in temporally extended and consequence-bearing environments.
