## 2. The Mutation Boundary Problem

The Mutation Boundary Problem is the problem of governing the transition between generative possibility and durable consequence in stateful opaque generative systems. It arises wherever a system is allowed not only to produce language, but also to participate across time in ways that alter persistent state, commitments, plans, relations, memories, records, or actions. In such systems, the critical question is not simply whether an output is locally plausible, but whether the path from output to consequence is sufficiently legible, bounded, and accountable to sustain continuity over time.

This problem appears when three conditions coincide. First, the system is stateful: something about its prior activity is allowed to persist and condition later behavior. Second, the system is opaque: the internal process by which it arrives at an output is not fully inspectable or reliably recoverable in a way that can bear governance by itself. Third, the system is generative: it produces outputs through probabilistic continuation rather than deterministic execution of a fully specified symbolic program. Each of these conditions matters, but it is their combination that creates the mutation boundary as a distinct problem. A deterministic workflow with explicit state transitions does not raise the same difficulty. Nor does a purely stateless text generator whose outputs have no durable effect. The problem emerges when stochastic generation is permitted to write itself into an ongoing world.

A foundational dependency must be stated explicitly, because the consequences of its failure extend far beyond this paper.

The Mutation Boundary Problem presupposes that generative systems are meaningfully responsive to semantic structure at the interface level — that language-native governance can constrain behavior because the system genuinely couples to the meaning-bearing structure of its input, not merely because outputs happen to correlate with instructions under favorable conditions. This is not a novel or unusual assumption. It is the same assumption on which prompt engineering, alignment research, constitutional methods, reinforcement learning from human feedback, orchestration frameworks, and every commercially deployed LLM application currently depends. This paper simply names it.

The stakes of that dependency deserve plain statement. If semantic coupling is not real — if apparent compliance is statistical artifact rather than genuine structural responsiveness — then the Mutation Boundary Problem is not merely unsolvable by the methods proposed here. It is ungovernable by any language-native method, and every system currently relying on such methods is operating on false premises.

But the implications do not stop there. If semantic governance is ruled out, the only remaining path to governed long-horizon agency is purely external enforcement: deterministic software systems that constrain every state transition, audit every output, and permit no mutation without mechanistic verification. Such systems are buildable in principle. In practice, the enforcement overhead required to govern a stochastic generator entirely from the outside — without any reliable semantic cooperation from the system itself — would be so comprehensive as to eliminate the adaptive, generative capacity that makes language models useful in the first place. The human labor cost of auditing alone would be astronomical. At that point, one has not governed an LLM. One has built a deterministic system with an expensive stochastic component that must be overridden at every consequential juncture.

The practical consequence is a forced choice. Either semantic coupling is real and the Mutation Boundary Problem is the central governance challenge to be addressed — in which case something structurally resembling semantic governance at the boundary, combined with staged external enforcement, is the necessary response — or semantic coupling is not real, and long-horizon autonomous agents in high-stakes, consequence-bearing domains are not viable under any architecture currently conceivable. There is no middle position in which coupling fails but long-horizon governance succeeds.

This paper proceeds on the assumption that coupling is real, because the alternative renders the entire field's current trajectory incoherent. But the assumption is testable, and this paper's own framework provides conditions under which it could be falsified. If it is falsified, the appropriate response is not to seek a better governance pattern. It is to recognize that the foundational capability the field has assumed does not exist.

The framing above identifies opacity as one of the three coinciding conditions. It is important to clarify, however, that opacity functions here as an aggravating condition rather than as the root cause of the mutation boundary problem. Even a fully transparent generator — one whose internal reasoning process is completely and continuously inspectable — would still face the mutation boundary problem in its essential form. Transparency provides a causal account of why a particular output was produced. It does not determine whether that output should become durable state, whether it is consistent with existing commitments, whether it violates structural invariants, whether it conflicts with other proposals in flight, whether it exceeds authorized step bounds, or whether it has been subjected to appropriate review before hardening into consequence. Those are governance questions, and they operate at the boundary, not at the interior. Interpretability research provides what one might call the autopsy: a more complete reconstruction of the generative process after the fact. Governance provides the institutional form: the rules under which generation may or may not cross the threshold into durable reality. These are distinct instruments serving distinct functions, and neither substitutes for the other. The mutation boundary problem therefore arises from the structural relationship between generation and commitment — from the fact that generators produce candidates, and candidates are not commitments — rather than from the fact that the generating process is hidden. Opacity intensifies the problem by removing the diagnostic access that would otherwise permit a finer-grained examination at the boundary. A perfectly transparent system operating without mutation boundary governance will still drift, thrash, silently overwrite its own prior commitments, and produce audit theater indistinguishable from genuine accountability — with the sole difference that an observer may watch it happen in real time. The visibility of the mechanism does not resolve the absence of the rule.

The word mutation is used here deliberately. It does not mean any change whatsoever, nor is it limited to software-state updates in the narrow engineering sense. It refers to any transition by which a generative system's output becomes part of a durable reality that later cycles must inherit, navigate, or act upon. A mutation may be a change to persistent memory, a shift in an internal belief or hypothesis structure, a revised goal state, a new relational commitment, a tool-mediated action with external effect, or a new record inserted into a shared system. The boundary in question is therefore not merely a storage boundary. It is the boundary at which exploratory generation hardens into consequential state.

The term governance is equally important. The Mutation Boundary Problem is not solved merely by making outputs visible or logging them after the fact. A transcript can preserve what happened without making the logic of change lawful. Governance in this context means that the transition from possibility to durability is constrained by explicit conditions: what kinds of state may change, under what operations, with what authority, under what evidentiary requirements, with what traceability, and with what mechanisms for review or suppression. In other words, governance concerns not only observability but legitimacy. A mutation may be perfectly visible and still remain arbitrary, unbounded, or incoherent.

This distinction matters because many current systems already preserve some history while still failing at the mutation boundary. A conversational agent may retrieve past notes yet continue to rewrite itself opportunistically. A tool-using agent may log its tool calls while still overclaiming what it knows. A character simulation may remember previous scenes while allowing transient moods to silently replace durable identity. A planning assistant may produce structured outputs while quietly converting speculation into standing commitments. These systems often appear more stable than they are because they retain traces of the past. But retained trace is not the same as governed transition. The mutation boundary problem begins exactly where continuity requires more than mere recall.

A useful way to see the problem is to distinguish between exploration, interpretation, and commitment. Generative systems are valuable precisely because they can explore many continuations, interpret ambiguous inputs, and produce flexible meanings under uncertainty. That capacity should not be confused with commitment authority. Exploration is plural and provisional; commitment is singular and consequential. Interpretation is often contestable; durable mutation cannot remain perpetually contestable if a shared world is to remain navigable. The mutation boundary problem is therefore the problem of deciding how, when, and under what rules exploratory and interpretive activity may become durable change.

This problem also clarifies why familiar framings, while necessary, are incomplete. Hallucination is one instance of a broader issue: an epistemically unbounded output can mutate shared reality if allowed to harden unchecked. Memory drift is another instance: stored continuity becomes destructive when mutation lacks lawful constraints. Tool misuse is another: an external act can be triggered without a properly governed transition from intention to authorization. In each case, what appears as a discrete failure mode can be re-described more fundamentally as a breakdown at the mutation boundary. This is why the problem proposed here is not meant to replace those familiar concerns, but to organize them under a more general structure.

The problem becomes sharper under long horizons. Short interactions can often hide weak governance because consequences remain local and recent context masks incoherence. Over extended operation, however, even small ungoverned changes accumulate. Identities begin to blur. Hypotheses harden into assumptions. Temporary stressors become permanent dispositions. Relational states drift. Memory records lose causal clarity. Tool-driven facts and model-generated inferences become difficult to separate. What initially seemed like "small errors" become distortions in the conditions of future interpretation. Thus, long-horizon operation does not merely scale the same problem. It qualitatively transforms it by making the costs of illegible mutation cumulative.

What gives this cumulative drift its structural significance is not merely the difficulty of tracing individual transitions, but the nature of what is required to prevent them. Maintaining a clear distinction between generative exploration and durable consequence is not a passive condition that a well-designed system can simply preserve by default. In physical systems, local order is sustained only by continuous work at the boundary between the ordered region and the disordered environment surrounding it; the moment that expenditure ceases, entropy reclaims the difference. Generative systems face an analogous constraint. Every cycle in which output candidates are allowed to harden into durable state without governed transition is a cycle in which no work is being done at the boundary — and the disorder accumulates not dramatically but incrementally, as hypotheses congeal into assumptions, conjectures become authoritative records, and provisional framings become inherited constraints that future cycles cannot easily identify as such. Local order in generative systems is not free, is not automatic, and is not a natural consequence of scale or capability. It is the product of continuous governed work at the boundary between generation and commitment. Stop paying the cost, and the order dissolves. The only question is how fast. This means the mutation boundary cannot be treated as optional engineering overhead that a sufficiently capable system can eventually transcend. It is the condition of coherence in any system where stochastic processes write into durable reality, and no increase in capability, interpretability, or memory capacity substitutes for the work being done at that boundary.

A further complication is that the mutation boundary is partly semantic before it is procedural. In opaque language-based systems, many of the relevant transitions are mediated first through meaning rather than formal execution. A model does not naturally distinguish between a conjecture, a memory, a plan, a belief, and a committed fact unless some architecture or protocol makes those distinctions carry operational weight. This means the problem cannot be solved purely at the level of external logging or downstream enforcement. The system must in some way inhabit, or be made to interact with, a legible distinction between what is being proposed and what is being treated as durable reality. This is one reason the paper later turns to issues of ontology, canonical reference, epistemic boundaries, and substrate coupling.

The Mutation Boundary Problem is therefore not reducible to one narrow engineering task. It is a composite problem at the intersection of representation, authority, continuity, uncertainty, and consequence. It asks what kinds of state exist, what kinds of changes count, who or what may authorize them, how they remain interpretable over time, and how systems can continue to act meaningfully without pretending to possess forms of certainty or determinism they do not in fact have. Put simply: if a stochastic opaque generator is allowed to matter across time, then the point at which its outputs become durable must itself become an object of design.

This paper does not assume that there is only one correct solution shape. On the contrary, part of the purpose of naming the Mutation Boundary Problem is to make room for comparative architectural responses. Some domains may require stricter deterministic externalization; others may tolerate more semantic flexibility. Some systems may centralize authority; others may distribute proposal generation more broadly while retaining a single commit mechanism. Some may be optimized for narrative coherence, others for analytical rigor or enterprise control. What unites them, however, is the same underlying need: to determine how opaque generative exploration becomes lawful, accountable mutation rather than silent drift.

One implication of this domain-relative character deserves emphasis. The mutation boundary cannot be governed without first constituting the state surface through the domain's own ontology. What things exist and are mutable in this domain? What counts as evidence here? What transitions are lawful? Who holds commit authority, and over what? These are not questions OTTC can answer from the inside of its rectangle. They are contributions that must come from domain expertise. Governance therefore presupposes a prior act of domain-ontology translation: the expert's knowledge of what the domain's durable state surfaces are, how they are structured, and what legitimacy conditions apply must be rendered in machine-legible form before any mutation governance architecture can operate within that domain. This makes domains not merely use cases for OTTC, but constitutional worlds — each with its own law surface, its own legitimacy conditions, its own account of what makes mutation lawful. OTTC provides the rectangle. The constitutional world of each domain must supply the square.

It should be noted that the mutation boundary, as defined here, is not exclusively an egress phenomenon. While this paper focuses primarily on the transition from generative output to durable consequence — the point at which exploratory generation hardens into shared reality — the same structural problem arises wherever external input enters a stateful opaque system and conditions its subsequent behavior without governed transition. A user's evaluative framing, a retrieved document's implicit authority, a tool result's apparent certainty — each can cross into a system's operative posture as silently and as consequentially as any ungoverned output can cross into shared state. The boundary is bidirectional. This paper addresses the egress side in depth and returns to the ingress dimension in Section 8, where the complementary problem is named and its distinct architectural requirements are identified.

The remaining sections build from this definition. The next step is to show why current framings, while often valuable, remain insufficient to capture the full structure of the problem. Only then can the major question clusters be decomposed in a way that supports future work.
