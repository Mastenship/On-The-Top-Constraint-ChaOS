# Abstract - The Mutation Boundary Problem

**A Research Agenda for Stateful Opaque Generative Systems**

Long-horizon generative systems do not fail only through inaccurate outputs or unsafe actions. They also fail when persistent state, identity, commitments, or shared context mutate in ways that become illegible, unbounded, or unauditable over time. Current discourse around large language models and agentic systems often treats capability, alignment, memory, prompting, and tool use as separable concerns, but under-specifies a deeper challenge: the conditions under which an opaque, stochastic, stateful generator can participate coherently in a shared world across time without requiring false claims of determinism or full interior transparency.

This paper names that challenge the **Mutation Boundary Problem**. It defines the problem as the governance of the boundary between generative exploration and durable state change in stateful opaque generative systems. Rather than proposing a final solution, the paper offers a research agenda. It decomposes the problem into major domains including ontology and persistent state, continuity and identity, epistemic boundaries, lawful mutation, memory and causality, error and anti-fragility, tool-mediated evidence, substrate coupling, staged externalization, multi-agent governance, and domain-specific success criteria. The paper also identifies the mutation boundary as bidirectional: the same structural problem that governs egress — how generation becomes durable consequence — arises symmetrically at ingress, where external framing enters a system's operative posture without governed transition.

The paper further argues that governed mutation requires prior ontology translation: a domain's vocabulary, state surfaces, evidentiary distinctions, and lawful transitions must be rendered in machine-legible form before the mutation boundary can be governed at all. Governance cannot regulate what it cannot address. Domains are not merely vocabularies; they are constitutional worlds that must be translated into the address space of an OTTC-compliant architecture before that architecture can produce lawful mutation within that domain. OTTC provides the rectangle — the nine invariant kernel axioms — and invites domain experts in consequence-bearing fields to build their own squares by translating their field's ontology into OTTC-governed form. Domain-expert constitutional discovery is identified as a research direction.

OTTC is introduced as one provisional governance response that attempts to address these tensions by regulating mutation at the transition between proposal and commitment. ChaOS is presented as a domain-specific narrative implementation that makes these pressures concrete. Neither is offered as a final theory of governed stochastic participation. Instead, both are treated as constructive examples that clarify the problem space and demonstrate how one architectural direction attempts to respond to it.

The paper argues that progress in this area requires shifting attention from raw output quality to the legibility, boundedness, and accountability of change over time. A narrative player-character domain is used as a motivating case because it compresses these questions into a high-resolution setting where continuity, perspective, and consequence are immediately visible. The ingress dimension of the mutation boundary is identified as a structurally symmetric but architecturally distinct challenge, one whose governance requirements — informed by maturation doctrine and the precedent of human institutional protocol — point toward externalized rather than in-model solutions.
