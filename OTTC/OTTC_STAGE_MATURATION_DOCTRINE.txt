=== SECTION: OTTC_STAGE_MATURATION_DOCTRINE ===

[KEEP: Title]
OTTC Stage Maturation Doctrine
Why Stage 1 Matters, Why Migration Is Necessary, and Why Stage 3 Cannot Be Skipped

=== SECTION_BREAK ===

=== SECTION: PURPOSE ===

[KEEP: Purpose]
This document codifies the OTTC doctrine of architectural maturation.

It answers three recurring errors:

1) STAGE_1_DISMISSAL_ERROR
- “If deterministic external governance is the real target, Stage 1 is unnecessary.”
- “Just build the software first.”

2) STAGE_1_ROMANTICISM_ERROR
- “If the machine works in-context, externalization is unnecessary.”
- “Just keep everything in the LLM forever.”

3) STAGE_3_SKIPPING_ERROR
- “We can externalize immediately without first discovering the lawful semantic interface the model can reliably inhabit.”

OTTC rejects all three errors.

The correct doctrine is:
- Stage 1 is necessary for constitutional discovery and coupling assay.
- Stage 2 is necessary for safe migration and mixed-boundary engineering.
- Stage 3 is necessary for best deterministic governance results in long-horizon, high-risk, or high-consequence systems.

Stage progression is maturation, not repudiation.

=== SECTION_BREAK ===

=== SECTION: CORE_THESIS ===

[[LLM_INSTRUCTION]]: Treat this as the governing thesis of the doctrine.

A deterministic governance layer cannot reliably govern what the stochastic generator cannot yet lawfully express.

Therefore:
- Stage 1 exists to discover and stabilize the constitutional language of proposals, audits, paths, invariants, and bounded role behavior inside the model’s native semantic medium.
- Stage 2 exists to externalize selected authorities and checks without breaking the learned constitutional interface.
- Stage 3 exists to move durable state, commit authority, verification, and quantitative replay analytics into deterministic systems once the semantic protocol is stable enough to support them.

Short form:
- Stage 1 discovers the law surface.
- Stage 2 migrates the law surface.
- Stage 3 enforces the law surface.

=== SECTION_BREAK ===

=== SECTION: FOUNDATIONAL_OBSERVATION ===

[KEEP: Observation]
OTTC is language-native governance.

Because OTTC constrains a probabilistic text policy through semantic law, the model must first be able to:
- recognize canonical addresses
- distinguish proposal from commit
- obey bounded operations
- preserve hard-vs-soft state separation
- emit complete audits
- remain within epistemic and role limits
- produce artifacts stable enough for deterministic systems to parse

If the model cannot do this reliably, then external governance software will not be governing a stable constitutional protocol.
It will instead be parsing unstable text behavior and treating extraction fragility as architecture.

This is why Stage 1 cannot be skipped.

=== SECTION_BREAK ===

=== SECTION: WHY_STAGE_1_EXISTS ===

[[LLM_INSTRUCTION]]: This section defines the purpose of Stage 1.

Stage 1 is the constitutional discovery and coupling assay phase.

Its purpose is not:
- final safety
- final determinism
- final reliability
- final production architecture

Its purpose is to determine whether the candidate model can stably inhabit an OTTC-compatible law surface at all, and if so, under what constraints.

Stage 1 is where you discover:

1) ONTOLOGY_FIT
- What persistent modules can be made legible?
- What tokens and record forms are stable?

2) DSL_FIT
- What syntax and segmentation does the model obey under stress?
- What canonical forms retain gravity over long contexts?

3) COUPLING_LIMITS
- Where does the model drift first?
- Which invariants are weakly held?
- Which roles should be reduced, split, or externalized?

4) AUTHORITY_FEASIBILITY
- Can this unit serve only as proposer?
- Can it also govern?
- Must audit or commit be reassigned?

5) FAILURE_SIGNATURES
- Does it alias?
- Does it perform illegal direct writes?
- Does it narrate beyond role?
- Does it produce audit theater?

6) LANGUAGE_AND_CULTURE_EFFECTS
- Does coupling change across intended language surfaces?

This is why qualification assay is a mandatory blind pre-build gate.
You test the ground before foundations.

=== SECTION_BREAK ===

=== SECTION: WHY_STAGE_1_IS_NOT_OPTIONAL_FLUFF ===

[KEEP: Rule]
Stage 1 is not “just prompting.”
Stage 1 is not “prototyping until software exists.”
Stage 1 is not “informal scaffolding.”

It is the only phase in which the operator can directly observe whether the candidate model can:
- inhabit canonical law
- separate proposal from reality
- preserve auditable causality
- and produce a parseable constitutional artifact language

without first being over-conditioned by a finished architecture.

A system that skips this phase risks:
- externalizing a bad ontology
- cementing unstable token choices
- building parsers for artifacts the model does not naturally hold
- mistaking conditioned post-build compliance for native coupling
- and investing engineering effort into a constitutional shape the model cannot support cleanly

Therefore:
Skipping Stage 1 does not save time in principle.
It often converts discovery debt into later architectural debt.

=== SECTION_BREAK ===

=== SECTION: WHY_STAGE_3_CANNOT_BE_SKIPPED_TO_IMMEDIATELY ===

[[LLM_INSTRUCTION]]: This section refutes the “just start with software” objection.

The common objection is:
- “If external deterministic governance is the target, why not start there immediately?”

Answer:
Because deterministic governance still requires a stable semantic interchange format.

External systems must receive artifacts such as:
- canonical proposals
- legal target paths
- operation types
- reason codes
- mechanism tags
- audit entries
- verification inputs
- tool-intent requests

These do not arise automatically from generic model output.
They must be discovered, stabilized, and assayed inside the model’s own semantic medium first.

Without Stage 1, software teams typically end up doing one or more of the following:
- parsing prose that was never constitutionally stable
- inventing schemas the model weakly inhabits
- overfitting parsers to one model/version
- collapsing semantic distinctions because the interface was not discovered first
- treating extraction success as governance success
- confusing formal syntax with effective coupling

Therefore:
You may build external software early, but you may not validly assume that you have skipped Stage 1 unless the model-facing constitutional protocol has already been discovered and qualified.

The sequence is not:
- software first, semantics later

The sequence is:
- assay and discover semantics first
- then externalize deterministically

=== SECTION_BREAK ===

=== SECTION: WHY_STAGE_2_EXISTS ===

[[LLM_INSTRUCTION]]: This section defines the role of the hybrid stage.

Stage 2 exists because migration from pure in-context governance to deterministic governance is rarely atomic.

Stage 2 is the hybridization phase.
Its purpose is to preserve the stable constitutional language discovered in Stage 1 while gradually moving authority, storage, and verification into deterministic systems.

Typical Stage 2 moves include:
- external state persistence
- external commit logging
- schema validation
- invariant checking
- canonical translation-before-commit
- tool-actuation mediation
- semantic-to-numeric downstream encoding
- role split across multiple models or humans

Stage 2 matters because:
- it exposes where the semantic interface survives contact with software
- it identifies which artifacts are parseable enough for hard enforcement
- it allows governance boundary placement to be adjusted by evidence
- it reduces the risk of losing substrate fit during externalization

A doctrine that lacks Stage 2 usually falls into one of two failure modes:
- indefinite Stage 1 romanticism
- premature Stage 3 overformalization

=== SECTION_BREAK ===

=== SECTION: WHY_STAGE_3_IS_NECESSARY ===

[[LLM_INSTRUCTION]]: This section defines the end-state rationale.

Stage 3 is necessary because language-native constraint alone does not create hard guarantees.

Over long horizons, in-model-only governance remains vulnerable to:
- context erosion
- salience loss
- instruction interference
- role confusion
- audit theater
- accidental write-surface bypass
- adversarial pressure
- numerical brittleness
- weak replay guarantees

Therefore the best deterministic governance results require that, eventually:
- persistent state lives outside the model
- commit authority is externalized
- verification is externalized
- tool actuation is externally mediated
- quantitative replay analytics operate on post-audit canonical artifacts

Stage 3 is not a rejection of OTTC’s language-native nature.
It is OTTC completed at the enforcement boundary.

The model remains:
- proposer
- interpreter
- rationale generator
- bounded subjective processor

The deterministic layer becomes:
- source of truth
- commit authority
- verifier
- audit enforcer
- quantitative analytics layer

=== SECTION_BREAK ===

=== SECTION: WHAT_PERSISTS_ACROSS_ALL_STAGES ===

[KEEP: Rule]
Stage progression changes where authority lives.
It does not change OTTC’s kernel identity.

Across all stages, OTTC still requires:
- canonical addressing
- propose→commit separation
- bounded ops
- hard-vs-soft separation
- step limits with override proof
- deterministic conflict resolution
- audit closure
- post-commit verification

What changes is:
- whether these are represented entirely in-model
- partially externalized
- or enforced by deterministic source-of-truth systems

Thus:
Stage progression is not replacement of OTTC.
It is increasing enforcement depth of OTTC.

=== SECTION_BREAK ===

=== SECTION: THE_DEVELOPMENTAL_LOGIC ===

[[LLM_INSTRUCTION]]: Preserve this order as doctrine.

NORMAL_MATURATION_ORDER:
1) QUALIFY the candidate unit in a blind pre-build assay
2) DISCOVER the lawful semantic DSL / ontology surface
3) BUILD the machine in text-native form
4) RETEST the built machine for regression
5) EXTERNALIZE the state, commit, and verification layers where risk or horizon requires
6) PRESERVE the same constitutional interface for model-to-system communication

This means:
- the model learns to speak constitutional law before software depends on it
- software is built around a discovered stable protocol rather than around wishful schema design
- deterministic governance receives canonical artifacts rather than unstable prose

=== SECTION_BREAK ===

=== SECTION: STAGE_1_FAILURE_MODE ===

[KEEP: Failure Mode]
If an architecture remains indefinitely in Stage 1 while:
- authority grows
- consequence grows
- horizon grows
- adversarial pressure grows

then governance debt is accumulating.

Symptoms:
- more law is added to compensate for missing enforcement
- audit grows theatrical under pressure
- context burden rises
- deterministic guarantees are claimed without deterministic authority
- operators begin trusting conditioned behavior as if it were runtime enforcement

Remedy:
- Migrate selected authorities into Stage 2/3 deterministic governance.

=== SECTION_BREAK ===

=== SECTION: STAGE_3_PREMATURE_FORMALIZATION_FAILURE_MODE ===

[KEEP: Failure Mode]
If an architecture attempts Stage 3 externalization before constitutional discovery is complete, common failures include:
- parser brittleness
- schema mismatch
- unstable canonical tokens
- overfit prompts bound to one model/version
- loss of domain nuance at the machine boundary
- governance software that enforces malformed abstractions
- expensive code commitments to a protocol the model never truly inhabited

Remedy:
- Return to Stage 1 qualification and DSL assay.
- Re-establish the lawful semantic protocol before hardening the deterministic boundary.

=== SECTION: WHY_THIS_DOCTRINE_IS_GENERAL ===

[KEEP: Rule]
This doctrine applies beyond ChaOS.

Any OTTC machine that depends on a stochastic semantic generator must answer:
- What is the lawful write surface?
- Can the generator reliably inhabit it?
- Which roles remain in-model?
- Which roles must migrate outward?
- At what risk tier does Stage 1 become insufficient?

Therefore the maturation doctrine is not domain-specific ornament.
It is a general engineering and governance principle for language-native constitutional systems.

=== SECTION_BREAK ===

=== SECTION: PRACTICAL_OPERATOR_RULES ===

[[LLM_INSTRUCTION]]: These rules are actionable and normative.

RULE_1:
Never treat post-build regression evidence as qualification evidence.

RULE_2:
Never claim Stage 3 governance if the model-facing constitutional interface was not first assayed and stabilized.

RULE_3:
Never confuse parseability with coupling.
A parser can extract unstable behavior; that does not make the protocol sound.

RULE_4:
Never confuse Stage 1 success with final safety.
In-model compliance is not deterministic enforcement.

RULE_5:
Never skip Stage 2 if migration changes artifact shape, authority placement, or verification behavior.
Hybridization exists to protect protocol continuity.

RULE_6:
As stakes rise, externalize:
- persistent state
- commit authority
- verification
- tool actuation
- downstream numeric analytics

RULE_7:
Preserve the constitutional language across migration.
Do not let software erase the semantic distinctions that made governance possible.

=== SECTION: RELATION_TO_SEMANTIC_ABACUS ===

[KEEP: Clarification]
The maturation doctrine and Semantic Abacus are mutually reinforcing.

Stage 1:
- discover the semantic beads the model can lawfully manipulate

Stage 2:
- preserve those beads while beginning downstream encoding and comparison

Stage 3:
- treat post-audit canonical semantics as the source for deterministic numeric indexing, thresholds, trend analysis, and replay metrics

Thus:
Semantic Abacus is not a Stage 3 replacement for Stage 1.
It depends on Stage 1 having first discovered stable semantic units worth encoding.

=== SECTION_BREAK ===

=== SECTION: RELATION_TO_CONTROLLED_TOOL_ACTUATION ===

[KEEP: Clarification]
The same maturation logic applies to tool use.

Stage 1:
- the model must first learn to express TOOL_INTENT as a lawful proposal artifact

Stage 2:
- governance mediates tool approval and logging with mixed in-model/external procedures

Stage 3:
- tool execution, provenance capture, and policy gates are deterministic and external

Therefore:
A controlled tool boundary cannot simply be “added by software” if the model has not first learned to communicate tool intent in a canonical constitutional format.

=== SECTION: SUMMARY_DOCTRINE ===

[[LLM_INSTRUCTION]]: Preserve this section as the shortest canonical compression.

OTTC Stage Doctrine:

- Stage 1 is for discovery.
- Stage 2 is for migration.
- Stage 3 is for enforcement.

You cannot safely remain forever at Stage 1.
You cannot validly skip directly to Stage 3.
You cannot build deterministic governance for a protocol the model cannot yet stably express.

Therefore:
assay first,
build second,
externalize third,
and preserve the constitutional interface across every stage.
